## Chapter 3: Network Design

### Open Systems Interconnection (OSI) Model

3.1. The OSI Reference Model establishes a peer-to-peer relationship between corresponding layers in the protocol stack of two systems.

3.2. Each OSI layer provides services to the layer above it.

3.3. Service Access Points (SAPs) are the connection points between OSI layers.

3.4. Upper layers in the OSI model providing services to lower layers is not a protocol stack characteristic.

3.5. OSI Layer 1 is the Physical layer.

3.6. OSI Layer 2 is the Data link layer.

3.7. OSI Layer 7 is the Application layer.

3.8. Data is transmitted from the lowest layer of the sender to the lowest layer of the receiver.

3.9. OSI Layer 2 is responsible for intranet work data transfer.

3.10. OSI Layer 4 allows various levels of quality to be assigned to the data transfer process.

3.11. OSI Layer 7 supports multiple applications (e.g., email software) using the same protocols.

3.12. The OSI model is designed to provide a structured approach for network development.

3.13. Changes can be made to any OSI layer without affecting other layers.

3.14. OSI layers are commonly visualized in a vertical form, called a protocol stack.

### Data Center Design

3.15. A local area network (LAN) typically covers a building or part of it.

3.16. A Class 4 data center has no single point of failure.

3.17. Class 4 data centers require 2 horizontal distribution areas (HDAs) per equipment distribution area.

3.18. Class 3 or higher data centers require multiple entrance rooms (ERs).

3.19. A Class 2 data center must have at least two access provider (AP) connections.

3.20. Data center entrance rooms served by two APs must be 20 m (82 ft) apart.

3.21. The end-of-row topology in computer rooms (CRs) uses less cabling than centralized topologies.

3.22. A centralized topology uses fewer switches compared to other CR topologies.

3.23. Class 4 systems without component redundancy require a 2(N+N) configuration.

3.24. A storage area network (SAN) can use a core storage director to ensure storage availability for all servers.

3.25. Virtual servers can be added, deleted, or transferred to optimize service delivery.

3.26. Unified communications (UC) integrates VoIP, video communications, instant messaging, and presence indicators.

### Network Design and Architecture

3.27. Physical network design is used when detailed network characteristics are unavailable.

3.28. Functional network design is also referred to as top-down design.

3.29. A network’s architecture includes hardware, software, and applications.

3.30. A full mesh WAN design links every site to every other site, avoiding single points of failure.

3.31. A partial mesh WAN design eliminates single points of failure.

3.32. A VLAN uses routers to divide a single broadcast domain into smaller ones.

3.33. Broadcast domains are also called subnets.

3.34. Network redundancy relies on both the telecommunications and hardware infrastructure.

3.35. An excessive number of broadcasts limits the devices connected to switches in a LAN.

3.36. A Layer 3 switch processes frames based on Layer 3 address information.

3.37. Layer 3 switches use hardware-based logic for faster routing but are less flexible than traditional routers.

3.38. Each port on a network switch represents a separate collision domain and serves as a dedicated connection.

3.39. Each switch port also acts as a separate communication channel.

3.40. Campus network cabling is part of backbone cabling.

3.41. PAN stands for Personal Area Network.

3.42. CAN stands for Campus Area Network.

3.43. MAN stands for Metropolitan Area Network.

3.44. WAN stands for Wide Area Network.

### Virtualization and Server Clusters

3.45. Virtualization allows multiple servers to operate within the same physical host while retaining benefits of separate physical servers.

3.46. Virtual servers save space and are easier to configure and maintain.

3.47. Server clusters enable failover access, transferring activities from a failed server to another.

3.48. High availability refers to the improved reliability achieved by server clustering.

### Network Components

3.49. A network interface card (NIC) connects a station to the network medium.

3.50. Drivers link a station’s NIC to the operating system for communication.

3.51. A typical data center network links multiple servers to a core switch via network interfaces.

3.52. A “flat” network refers to a single broadcast domain spanning all switches and hubs.

3.53. Group videoconferencing is an example of point-to-multipoint bidirectional communication.

3.54. Videoconferencing is the most resource-demanding network application.

## Chapter 4: Telecommunications Spaces

### General Design Requirements

4.1. Telecommunications spaces require at least 1 m (3.28 ft) of working clearance in front and rear of installed equipment.

4.2. Carpet is not recommended as a floor covering in telecommunications spaces.

4.3. Doors for telecommunications spaces should have the same fire rating as the room.

4.4. Double doors with removable center posts are permitted in telecommunications spaces.

4.5. For large equipment deliveries, double doors measuring 1.83 m (6 ft) wide and 2.3 m (90 in) high are recommended.

4.6. Entryways for equipment delivery must be at least 0.91 m (3 ft) wide.

4.7. Telecommunications spaces with load-bearing walls reduce relocation possibilities if the building expands.

### HVAC and Lighting

4.8. HVAC sensors in equipment rooms should be placed 1.52 m (5 ft) above finished floor (AFF).

4.9. If the building’s HVAC system cannot ensure continuous operation, a stand-alone HVAC unit with independent controls is required.

4.10. Recommended lighting includes 200 lux (18.6 foot-candles) in vertical planes 1 m (3.28 ft) AFF in aisles.

4.11. Recommended lighting includes 500 lux (46 foot-candles) in horizontal planes 1 m (3.28 ft) AFF in aisles.

### Equipment and Rack Placement

4.12. Racks, cabinets, and enclosures should be fed from electrical subpanels with overcurrent protection devices.

4.13. Racks, cabinets, or enclosures should be 2.1 m (7 ft) tall.

4.14. Equipment racks with angled patch panels should use vertical cable management hardware.

4.15. Recommendations include leaving 150 mm (6 in) of depth for wall-mounted equipment.

4.16. Noise-generating equipment in telecommunications spaces should be enclosed with sound barriers.

### Cable Pathways and Terminations

4.17. Cable trays are recommended for cable pathways in equipment rooms and should have a minimum of 305 mm (12 in) of access headroom.

4.18. Use cable trays under access flooring in equipment rooms.

4.19. Sleeves and slots in telecommunications spaces must not be left open after cable installation.

4.20. Conduits in ceilings should protrude 25.4 mm (1 in) to 51 mm (2 in) into the room without bends.

4.21. 4 trade size (103 mm) sleeves are standard for backbone pathways.

4.22. The recommended space for cross-connections with surge protection is 2-4 times greater than without surge protection.

4.23. Termination hardware should be mounted no closer than 152 mm (6 in) to corners.

### Floor Space and Sizing Guidelines

4.24. Equipment rooms (ERs) should serve a building, campus, tenant, or service provider.

4.25. Telecommunications spaces require a ceiling height of at least 2.4 m (8 ft) AFF.

4.26. For larger spaces, a ceiling height of at least 3 m (10 ft) AFF is recommended.

4.27. Minimum ER floor space is 12 m² (120 ft²) for buildings up to 50,000 m² (500,000 ft²).

4.28. ER size should increase by 1 m² (10 ft²) for every additional 10,000 m² (100,000 ft²).

4.29. ER minimum size can be determined by multiplying the number of work areas by 0.07 m² (0.75 ft²).

4.30. ER minimum size can be determined by multiplying the number of BAS devices by 0.023 m² (0.25 ft²).

4.31. For buildings with <93 m² (1000 ft²) usable floor space, self-contained cabinets may be used instead of a full ER.

4.32. For <200 work areas, ER size should be at least 14 m² (150 ft²).

4.33. Telecommunications spaces serving 750 m² (8070 ft²) should have dimensions of 3 m x 3.4 m (10 ft x 11 ft).

4.34. A telecommunications space of 3 m x 2.4 m (10 ft x 8 ft) can serve areas ≤465 m² (5000 ft²).

### Specific Design Considerations

4.35. BAS or building system equipment may be located in ERs.

4.36. ERs may provide main, intermediate, and horizontal cross-connects.

4.37. Telecommunications rooms (TRs) are required on every building floor.

4.38. In TRs with substantial backbone cable distribution, additional space should be included for splicing and ladder racking.

4.39. In multi-tenant buildings, authorization for entry into locked telecommunications spaces must be provided to all tenants.

### Grounding and Fire Safety

4.40. Telecommunications spaces must provide copper busbars for grounding if multiple equipment grounding or intersystem grounding is needed.

4.41. Ensure a clearance of ≥457 mm (18 in) below fire sprinkler heads for all objects.

4.42. Plywood used to line walls should be 2.4 m (8 ft) tall and at least 19 mm (0.75 in) thick.

### Other Recommendations

4.43. Housekeeping pads should elevate equipment at least 150 mm (6 in) off the floor in areas prone to water ingress.

4.44. Mount cross-connects as near as possible to the end of backbone pathways.

4.45. Duplex or quad power receptacles must be installed at least 152 mm (6 in) AFF.

4.46. Termination space for cable estimates: 1290 mm² (2 in²) for each optical fiber strand.

4.47. Termination space for cable estimates: 2580 mm² (4 in²) for each 4-pair circuit.

4.48. Use colored covers for cross-connect fields for color coding.

## Chapter 5: Backbone Distribution Systems

### Cabling Topologies

5.1. Mesh is not a fundamental cabling topology.

5.2. The star topology is the best design for building backbones.

5.3. The topology generally used for OSP cabling is the star topology.

5.4. A fully connected mesh topology is commonly used in enterprise networks for router connections.

5.5. A hierarchical star campus backbone uses interbuilding or intrabuilding links between the MC (CD) and IC (BD).

5.6. Logical topologies like bus, ring, and tree can be implemented within a physical star topology.

5.7. Using a star topology for campus backbones introduces single points of failure.

### Backbone Distribution System Components

5.8. A backbone distribution system provides connections between telecommunications spaces.

5.9. The main cross-connect (MC/CD) connects entrance cables, first-level backbone cables, and equipment cables and is also called a campus distributor.

5.10. The intermediate cross-connect (IC) is also called a building distributor.

5.11. The horizontal cross-connect (HC) is also called a floor distributor.

5.12. Campus backbone cabling offers the most design options for ICT designers.

5.13. Bridged taps are not allowed in building backbone cabling.

5.14. Balanced twisted-pair and optical fiber cabling may be combined under a single sheath.

5.15. Direct connections between HCs/FDs should only be used in specific circumstances.

5.16. Building distributors (ICs) are not required for small buildings with only one HC/FD.

5.17. The entrance facility (EF) provides public/private network service cable access to a building.

5.18. Indoor hardware in a backbone system is typically used for cable terminations.

### Backbone Pathways and Slots

5.19. Slots used in backbone systems should have a 25 mm (1 in) high curb.

5.20. Slots should be flush against the wall with a minimum depth of 150 mm (6 in) perpendicular to the wall.

5.21. Slots should be sized to increase by 0.008 m² (12 in²) for every 3716 m² (40,000 ft²) increase in usable floor space served.

5.22. Cable trays can be used as vertical pathways between vertically aligned telecommunications rooms.

5.23. Vertically aligned TRs with connecting sleeves or slots are the most common backbone pathway type.

### Sleeves and Sizing Recommendations

5.24. Sleeves used in backbone systems should extend 25 mm (1 in) above floor level.

5.25. Provisioning sleeves for 1858 m² (20,000 ft²) requires 5 sleeves.

5.26. Provisioning sleeves for 9290 m² (100,000 ft²) and 11,148 m² (120,000 ft²) requires 7 sleeves.

5.27. Sleeve quantity and size depend on usable floor space served by the backbone system.

### Backbone Cable Types and Standards

5.28. Category 3 multi-pair backbone cable is available in configurations from 4 to 2700 pairs.

5.29. Category 5e/class D twisted-pair cables support up to 100 MHz.

5.30. Category 6A/class EA twisted-pair cables support up to 500 MHz.

5.31. Class E twisted-pair cables support up to 250 MHz.

5.32. The maximum length for balanced twisted-pair backbone cables between network equipment is 100 m (328 ft).

5.33. The recommended multimode optical fiber for backbone cabling is OM4 or higher.

5.34. Single mode optical fiber is suitable for both analog and digital transmission in backbones.

### Optical Fiber Standards

5.35. ITU-T G.653 specifies dispersion-shifted single mode fiber for passive optical networks (PONs).

5.36. ITU-T G.655 specifies non-zero dispersion-shifted single mode fiber for PONs.

5.37. ITU-T G.655 is used for submarine and long-haul terrestrial applications.

### Ethernet Passive Optical Networks (EPONs)

5.38. The aggregation device in an EPON is the optical line terminal (OLT).

5.39. The OLT supports a minimum of 16 subscribers per port using passive optical splitters.

5.40. The optical network unit (ONU) connects the service provider’s fiber to the subscriber’s premises.

### Additional Backbone Distribution Notes

5.41. In a typical hierarchical star, MC (CD) links to IC (BD) and HCs (FDs).

5.42. The MC (CD) should ideally be co-located in the ER.

5.43. Backbone systems should have no more than two levels of cross-connections.

5.44. Direct connections between TRs for bus or ring configurations are additional to star topology connections.

5.45. Most common applications for optical fiber backbones involve multiplexed transmission.

5.46. Rigid metal conduit (RMC) is the heaviest and thickest-walled steel conduit.

5.47. Threads on conduit ends use industry-standard color coding: Yellow for Intermediate metal conduit (IMC).

5.48. Threads on conduit ends use industry-standard color coding: Red for Rigid metal conduit (RMC).

5.49. Tree and branch topology “branches” are called distribution cables in telephony.

5.50. In clustered star configurations, equipment concentration improves network management efficiency.

## Chapter 6: Horizontal Distribution Systems

### General Horizontal Cabling Guidelines

6.1. A horizontal cabling system extends from the work area telecommunications outlet/connector (TO) to the HC (FD).

6.2. The permanent link in a horizontal cabling channel extends from the HC (FD) to the TO/connector.

6.3. Each TO/connector in a horizontal cabling system is cabled directly to the HC (FD), except when connecting to under carpet cabling via a transition point.

6.4. A maximum of four connection points are allowed in a horizontal cabling channel model.

6.5. TO boxes should be installed at the same height as electrical outlets.

### Pathway Design and Installation

6.6. Horizontal pathways are categorized as continuous or non-continuous.

6.7. Examples of non-continuous pathways include J-hooks, with spacing between supports.

6.8. For horizontal pathways using access floors, a minimum of 50 mm (2 in) free space should be available above underfloor cable trays.

6.9. In ceiling-based pathways, allow at least 75 mm (3 in) of clear vertical space above conduits and cables.

6.10. No bend in a conduit system should exceed 90 degrees.

6.11. If a conduit run has more than two 90-degree bends, include a pull box or pull point.

6.12. The minimum pull box length should be 16 times the diameter of the largest conduit.

6.13. For perimeter raceways, use them only in dry locations.

6.14. Wireways/gutters provide more pulling space than pull boxes in conduit-based pathways.

6.15. Elbows, reducers, crossovers, and tees are fittings used to adjust cable tray direction or size.

### MUTOA and Consolidation Points

6.16. A MUTOA may serve a maximum of 12 work areas.

6.17. In MUTOA designs, derating factor for 26 AWG cords is 50%.

6.18. In MUTOA designs, derating factor for 28 AWG cords is 70%.

6.19. The work area side of a MUTOA should be marked with the maximum allowable equipment cord length.

6.20. A CP is an interconnection point within a horizontal cabling system.

### Passive Optical Networks (PONs)

6.21. PONs use simplex, single mode optical fiber infrastructure.

6.22. Optical splitters in PONs are unpowered and typically have a 1:32 splitter output ratio.

6.23. APC connectors mitigate back-reflection in PON systems.

6.24. Most PON installations do not include networking equipment in telecommunications spaces.

6.25. The aggregation device in a PON is the optical line terminal (OLT).

6.26. The maximum distance for ITU G.984 GPON (C optics) is 60 km (37 mi).

6.27. The maximum distance for ITU G.987 10GPON/XG-PON is 40 km (25 mi).

6.28. 10 Gb/s PONs require connectors to reduce back-reflection above the 1500 nm wavelength.

### PoE and Cabling Characteristics

6.29. Balanced twisted-pair cabling for PoE power delivery in BAS applications does not require specific conductor sizes.

6.30. Shielded cables for PoE exhibit less heat buildup than unshielded cables.

6.31. The maximum current per conductor for PoE Type 1 is 175 mA.

6.32. The maximum power at source for PoE Type 4 is 90 W.

### Balanced Twisted-Pair Cabling

6.33. T568A Blue pair is on pins 4 and 5.

6.34. T568A Orange pair is on pins 3 and 6.

6.35. T568B Orange pair is on pins 1 and 2.

6.36. 28 AWG patch cords have higher insertion loss than 26 AWG cords.

6.37. Balanced twisted-pair cords with stranded conductors exhibit higher attenuation than solid conductors.

### Access Floor Systems

6.38. Low-profile access floors are less than 150 mm (6 in) high.

6.39. Standard-height access floors in general office areas require 200 mm (8 in) clearance above the structural floor.

6.40. Medium-duty access floors should have uniform load capacity of 4.8 kPa (100 lbf/ft²).

6.41. Medium-duty access floors should have concentrated load capacity of 8.8 kN (2000 lbf).

6.42. Freestanding access floors are limited to finished heights of ≤305 mm (12 in).

### Optical Fiber Specifications and Testing

6.43. Optical fiber cabling for PONs should be tested for bi-directional loss at 1310 nm and 1550 nm wavelengths.

6.44. APC connectors are used to mitigate reflected light in PON systems.

6.45. For optical fiber slack in the TR, store 3 m (10 ft) inside fiber hardware.

### Additional Design Considerations

6.46. Horizontal cabling pathways should first focus on the pathway system design.

6.47. BAS pathways should anticipate one cable per system or coverage area.

6.48. Cable trays and wireways are typically supported at 1.5 m (5 ft) centers unless designed for longer spans.

6.49. Conduit runs for horizontal pathways should follow direct routes parallel to building lines.

6.50. A third conduit bend may be acceptable without derating capacity if the conduit size is increased.

6.51. Underfloor conduit systems have limited flexibility for cable changes.

## Chapter 7: ICT Cables and Connecting

### Optical Fiber Cabling and Applications
