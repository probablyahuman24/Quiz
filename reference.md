Study Notes on Chapter 3 \- Network Design   
Open Systems Interconnection (OSI) Model   
1\. The OSI Reference Model establishes a peer-to-peer relationship between corresponding layers in the protocol   
stack of two systems.   
2\. Each OSI layer provides services to the layer above it.   
3\. Service Access Points (SAPs) are the connection points between OSI layers.   
4\. Upper layers in the OSI model provide services to lower layers is not a protocol stack characteristic.   
5\. OSI layers can be described by a name or a number:   
o Layer 1: Physical layer.   
o Layer 2: Data link layer.   
o Layer 7: Application layer.   
6\. Data is transmitted from the lowest layer of the sender to the lowest layer of the receiver.   
7\. OSI Layer 2 is responsible for intranet work data transfer.   
8\. OSI Layer 4 allows various levels of quality to be assigned to the data transfer process.   
9\. OSI Layer 7 supports multiple applications (e.g., email software) using the same protocols.   
10\. The OSI model is designed to provide a structured approach for network development.   
11\. Changes can be made to any OSI layer without affecting other layers.   
12\. OSI layers are commonly visualized in a vertical form, called a protocol stack. 

 

Data Center Design   
13\. A local area network (LAN) typically covers a building or part of it.   
14\. A Class 4 data center has no single point of failure.   
15\. Class 4 data centers require 2 horizontal distribution areas (HDAs) per equipment distribution area.   
16\. Class 3 or higher data centers require multiple entrance rooms (ERs).   
17\. A Class 2 data center must have at least two access provider (AP) connections. 

 

 

18\. Data center entrance rooms served by two APs must be 20 m (82 ft) apart.   
19\. The end-of-row topology in computer rooms (CRs) uses less cabling than centralized topologies.   
20\. A centralized topology uses fewer switches compared to other CR topologies.   
21\. Class 4 systems without component redundancy require a 2(N+N) configuration.   
22\. A storage area network (SAN) can use a core storage director to ensure storage availability for all servers.   
23\. Virtual servers can be added, deleted, or transferred to optimize service delivery.   
24\. Unified communications (UC) integrates VoIP, video communications, instant messaging, and presence   
indicators. 

 

Network Design and Architecture   
25\. Physical network design is used when detailed network characteristics are unavailable.   
26\. Functional network design is also referred to as top-down design.   
27\. A network's architecture includes hardware, software, and applications.   
28\. A full mesh WAN design links every site to every other site, avoiding single points of failure.   
29\. A partial mesh WAN design eliminates single points of failure.   
30\. A VLAN uses routers to divide a single broadcast domain into smaller ones.   
31\. Broadcast domains are also called subnets.   
32\. Network redundancy relies on both the telecommunications and hardware infrastructure.   
33\. An excessive number of broadcasts limits the devices connected to switches in a LAN.   
34\. A Layer 3 switch processes frames based on Layer 3 address information.   
35\. Layer 3 switches use hardware-based logic for faster routing but are less flexible than traditional routers.   
36\. Each port on a network switch represents a separate collision domain and serves as a dedicated connection.   
37\. Each switch port also acts as a separate communication channel.   
38\. Campus network cabling is part of backbone cabling. 

 

 

39\. Data network types:   
o PAN: Personal Area Network.   
o CAN: Campus Area Network.   
o MAN: Metropolitan Area Network.   
o WAN: Wide Area Network. 

 

Virtualization and Server Clusters   
40\. Virtualization allows multiple servers to operate within the same physical host while retaining benefits of   
separate physical servers.   
41\. Virtual servers save space and are easier to configure and maintain.   
42\. Server clusters enable failover access, transferring activities from a failed server to another.   
43\. High availability refers to the improved reliability achieved by server clustering. 

 

Network Components 

 

 

44\. A network interface card (NIC) connects a station to the network medium.   
45\. Drivers link a station’s NIC to the operating system for communication.   
46\. A typical data center network links multiple servers to a core switch via network interfaces.   
47\. A "flat" network refers to a single broadcast domain spanning all switches and hubs.   
48\. Group videoconferencing is an example of point-to-multipoint bidirectional communication.   
49\. Videoconferencing is the most resource-demanding network application. 

 

Additional Notes   
50\. MANs link networks across multiple sites within a city.   
51\. A typical WAN links networks over distances that span beyond a single city.   
52\. A network's framework for message exchange was developed by ISO.   
53\. A server is a computing device managing shared resources on a network.   
54\. Data centers benefit from storage and processing maximization through virtualization.   
Study Notes on Chapter 4 \- Telecommunications Spaces   
General Design Requirements   
1\. Telecommunications spaces require at least 1 m (3.28 ft) of working clearance in front and rear of installed   
equipment.   
2\. Carpet is not recommended as a floor covering in telecommunications spaces.   
3\. Doors for telecommunications spaces should have the same fire rating as the room.   
4\. Double doors with removable center posts are permitted in telecommunications spaces.   
5\. For large equipment deliveries, double doors measuring 1.83 m (6 ft) wide and 2.3 m (90 in) high are   
recommended.   
6\. Entryways for equipment delivery must be at least 0.91 m (3 ft) wide.   
7\. Telecommunications spaces with load-bearing walls reduce relocation possibilities if the building expands. 

 

HVAC and Lighting   
8\. HVAC sensors in equipment rooms should be placed 1.52 m (5 ft) above finished floor (AFF).   
9\. If the building's HVAC system cannot ensure continuous operation, a stand-alone HVAC unit with   
independent controls is required.   
10\. Lighting recommendations include:   
o 200 lux (18.6 foot-candles) in vertical planes 1 m (3.28 ft) AFF in aisles.   
o 500 lux (46 foot-candles) in horizontal planes 1 m (3.28 ft) AFF in aisles. 

 

Equipment and Rack Placement   
11\. Racks, cabinets, and enclosures should be fed from electrical subpanels with overcurrent protection devices.   
12\. Racks, cabinets, or enclosures should be 2.1 m (7 ft) tall.   
13\. Equipment racks with angled patch panels should use vertical cable management hardware.   
14\. Recommendations include leaving 150 mm (6 in) of depth for wall-mounted equipment.   
15\. Noise-generating equipment in telecommunications spaces should be enclosed with sound barriers. 

 

 

 

Cable Pathways and Terminations   
16\. Cable trays are recommended for cable pathways in equipment rooms and should have a minimum of 305 mm   
(12 in) of access headroom.   
17\. Use cable trays under access flooring in equipment rooms.   
18\. Sleeves and slots in telecommunications spaces must not be left open after cable installation.   
19\. Conduits in ceilings should protrude 25.4 mm (1 in) to 51 mm (2 in) into the room without bends.   
20\. Recommendations for sleeves:   
o 4 trade size (103 mm) sleeves are standard for backbone pathways.   
21\. The recommended space for cross-connections with surge protection is 2-4 times greater than without surge   
protection.   
22\. Termination hardware should be mounted no closer than 152 mm (6 in) to corners. 

 

Floor Space and Sizing Guidelines   
23\. Equipment rooms (ERs) should serve a building, campus, tenant, or service provider.   
24\. Telecommunications spaces require a ceiling height of at least 2.4 m (8 ft) AFF.   
25\. For larger spaces, a ceiling height of at least 3 m (10 ft) AFF is recommended.   
26\. ER floor space guidelines:   
o Minimum 12 m² (120 ft²) for buildings up to 50,000 m² (500,000 ft²).   
o Increase size by 1 m² (10 ft²) for every additional 10,000 m² (100,000 ft²).   
27\. ER minimum size can be determined by:   
o Multiplying the number of work areas by 0.07 m² (0.75 ft²).   
o Multiplying the number of BAS devices by 0.023 m² (0.25 ft²).   
28\. For buildings with \<93 m² (1000 ft²) usable floor space, self-contained cabinets may be used instead of a full   
ER.   
29\. For \<200 work areas, ER size should be at least 14 m² (150 ft²).   
30\. Telecommunications spaces serving 750 m² (8070 ft²) should have dimensions of 3 m x 3.4 m (10 ft x 11 ft).   
31\. A telecommunications space of 3 m x 2.4 m (10 ft x 8 ft) can serve areas ≤465 m² (5000 ft²). 

 

Specific Design Considerations   
32\. BAS or building system equipment may be located in ERs.   
33\. ERs may provide main, intermediate, and horizontal cross-connects.   
34\. Telecommunications rooms (TRs) are required on every building floor.   
35\. In TRs with substantial backbone cable distribution, additional space should be included for splicing and   
ladder racking.   
36\. In multi-tenant buildings, authorization for entry into locked telecommunications spaces must be provided to   
all tenants. 

 

 

 

 

 

Grounding and Fire Safety   
37\. Telecommunications spaces must provide copper busbars for grounding if multiple equipment grounding or   
intersystem grounding is needed.   
38\. Ensure a clearance of ≥457 mm (18 in) below fire sprinkler heads for all objects.   
39\. Plywood used to line walls should be 2.4 m (8 ft) tall and at least 19 mm (0.75 in) thick. 

 

Other Recommendations   
40\. Housekeeping pads should elevate equipment at least 150 mm (6 in) off the floor in areas prone to water   
ingress.   
41\. Mount cross-connects as near as possible to the end of backbone pathways.   
42\. Duplex or quad power receptacles must be installed at least 152 mm (6 in) AFF.   
43\. Termination space for cable estimates:   
o 1290 mm² (2 in²) for each optical fiber strand.   
o 2580 mm² (4 in²) for each 4-pair circuit.   
44\. Use colored covers for cross-connect fields for color coding.

Cabling Topologies

2\. The star topology is the best design for building backbones.   
3\. The topology generally used for OSP cabling is the star topology.   
4\. A fully connected mesh topology is commonly used in enterprise networks for router connections.   
5\. A hierarchical star campus backbone uses interbuilding or intrabuilding links between the MC (CD) and IC   
(BD).   
6\. Logical topologies like bus, ring, and tree can be implemented within a physical star topology.   
7\. Using a star topology for campus backbones introduces single points of failure. 

 

Backbone Distribution System Components   
8\. A backbone distribution system provides connections between telecommunications spaces.   
9\. The main cross-connect (MC/CD) connects entrance cables, first-level backbone cables, and equipment cables   
and is also called a campus distributor.   
10\. The intermediate cross-connect (IC) is also called a building distributor.   
11\. The horizontal cross-connect (HC) is also called a floor distributor.   
12\. Campus backbone cabling offers the most design options for ICT designers.   
13\. Bridged taps are not allowed in building backbone cabling.   
14\. Balanced twisted-pair and optical fiber cabling may be combined under a single sheath.   
15\. Direct connections between HCs/FDs should only be used in specific circumstances.   
16\. Building distributors (ICs) are not required for small buildings with only one HC/FD.   
17\. The entrance facility (EF) provides public/private network service cable access to a building.   
18\. Indoor hardware in a backbone system is typically used for cable terminations. 

 

 

 

Backbone Pathways and Slots   
19\. Slots used in backbone systems should have a 25 mm (1 in) high curb.   
20\. Slots should be flush against the wall with a minimum depth of 150 mm (6 in) perpendicular to the wall.   
21\. Slots should be sized to increase by 0.008 m² (12 in²) for every 3716 m² (40,000 ft²) increase in usable floor   
space served.   
22\. Cable trays can be used as vertical pathways between vertically aligned telecommunications rooms.   
23\. Vertically aligned TRs with connecting sleeves or slots are the most common backbone pathway type. 

 

Sleeves and Sizing Recommendations   
24\. Sleeves used in backbone systems should extend 25 mm (1 in) above floor level.   
25\. Provisioning sleeves for floors:   
o 5 sleeves for 1858 m² (20,000 ft²).   
o 7 sleeves for 9290 m² (100,000 ft²) and 11,148 m² (120,000 ft²).   
26\. Sleeve quantity and size depend on usable floor space served by the backbone system. 

 

Backbone Cable Types and Standards   
27\. Category 3 multi-pair backbone cable is available in configurations from 4 to 2700 pairs.   
28\. Category 5e/class D twisted-pair cables support up to 100 MHz.   
29\. Category 6A/class EA twisted-pair cables support up to 500 MHz.   
30\. Class E twisted-pair cables support up to 250 MHz.   
31\. The maximum length for balanced twisted-pair backbone cables between network equipment is 100 m (328 ft).   
32\. The recommended multimode optical fiber for backbone cabling is OM4 or higher.   
33\. Single mode optical fiber is suitable for both analog and digital transmission in backbones. 

 

Optical Fiber Standards   
34\. ITU-T G.653 specifies dispersion-shifted single mode fiber for passive optical networks (PONs).   
35\. ITU-T G.655 specifies non-zero dispersion-shifted single mode fiber for PONs.   
36\. ITU-T G.655 is used for submarine and long-haul terrestrial applications. 

 

Ethernet Passive Optical Networks (EPONs)   
37\. The aggregation device in an EPON is the optical line terminal (OLT).   
38\. The OLT supports a minimum of 16 subscribers per port using passive optical splitters.   
39\. The optical network unit (ONU) connects the service provider’s fiber to the subscriber’s premises. 

 

 

 

Additional Backbone Distribution Notes   
40\. In a typical hierarchical star, MC (CD) links to IC (BD) and HCs (FDs).   
41\. The MC (CD) should ideally be co-located in the ER.   
42\. Backbone systems should have no more than two levels of cross-connections.   
43\. Direct connections between TRs for bus or ring configurations are additional to star topology connections.   
44\. Most common applications for optical fiber backbones involve multiplexed transmission.   
45\. Rigid metal conduit (RMC) is the heaviest and thickest-walled steel conduit.   
46\. Threads on conduit ends use industry-standard color coding:   
o Yellow: Intermediate metal conduit (IMC).

47\. Tree and branch topology “branches” are called distribution cables in telephony.   
48\. In clustered star configurations, equipment concentration improves network management efficiency.

Study Notes on chapter 6 \- Horizontal Distribution Systems   
General Horizontal Cabling Guidelines   
1\. A horizontal cabling system extends from the work area telecommunications outlet/connector (TO) to the HC   
(FD).   
2\. The permanent link in a horizontal cabling channel extends from the HC (FD) to the TO/connector.   
3\. Each TO/connector in a horizontal cabling system is cabled directly to the HC (FD), except when connecting   
to under carpet cabling via a transition point.   
4\. A maximum of four connection points are allowed in a horizontal cabling channel model.   
5\. TO boxes should be installed at the same height as electrical outlets. 

 

Pathway Design and Installation   
6\. Horizontal pathways are categorized as continuous or non-continuous.   
7\. Examples of non-continuous pathways include J-hooks, with spacing between supports.   
8\. For horizontal pathways using access floors, a minimum of 50 mm (2 in) free space should be available above   
underfloor cable trays.   
9\. In ceiling-based pathways, allow at least 75 mm (3 in) of clear vertical space above conduits and cables.   
10\. For conduit systems:   
o No bend should exceed 90 degrees.   
o If a conduit run has more than two 90-degree bends, include a pull box or pull point.   
o The minimum pull box length should be 16 times the diameter of the largest conduit.   
11\. For perimeter raceways, use them only in dry locations.   
12\. Wireways/gutters provide more pulling space than pull boxes in conduit-based pathways.   
13\. Elbows, reducers, crossovers, and tees are fittings used to adjust cable tray direction or size. 

 

MUTOA and Consolidation Points   
14\. A MUTOA may serve a maximum of 12 work areas. 

 

 

15\. In MUTOA designs, derating factors for equipment cords are:   
o 50% for 26 AWG cords.   
o 70% for 28 AWG cords.   
16\. The work area side of a MUTOA should be marked with the maximum allowable equipment cord length.   
17\. A CP is an interconnection point within a horizontal cabling system. 

 

Passive Optical Networks (PONs)   
18\. PONs use simplex, single mode optical fiber infrastructure.   
19\. Optical splitters in PONs are unpowered and typically have a 1:32 splitter output ratio.   
20\. APC connectors mitigate back-reflection in PON systems.   
21\. Most PON installations do not include networking equipment in telecommunications spaces.   
22\. The aggregation device in a PON is the optical line terminal (OLT).   
23\. The maximum distance for ITU G.984 GPON (C optics) is 60 km (37 mi).   
24\. The maximum distance for ITU G.987 10GPON/XG-PON is 40 km (25 mi).   
25\. 10 Gb/s PONs require connectors to reduce back-reflection above the 1500 nm wavelength. 

 

PoE and Cabling Characteristics   
26\. Balanced twisted-pair cabling for PoE power delivery in BAS applications does not require specific conductor   
sizes.   
27\. Shielded cables for PoE exhibit less heat buildup than unshielded cables.   
28\. The maximum current per conductor for PoE Type 1 is 175 mA.   
29\. The maximum power at source for PoE Type 4 is 90 W. 

 

Balanced Twisted-Pair Cabling   
30\. T568A pin assignments:   
o Blue pair: Pins 4 and 5\.   
o Orange pair: Pins 3 and 6\.   
31\. T568B pin assignments:   
o Orange pair: Pins 1 and 2\.   
32\. 28 AWG patch cords have higher insertion loss than 26 AWG cords.   
33\. Balanced twisted-pair cords with stranded conductors exhibit higher attenuation than solid conductors. 

 

Access Floor Systems   
34\. Low-profile access floors are less than 150 mm (6 in) high.   
35\. Standard-height access floors in general office areas require 200 mm (8 in) clearance above the structural floor.   
36\. Medium-duty access floors should have:   
o Uniform load capacity of 4.8 kPa (100 lbf/ft²).   
o Concentrated load capacity of 8.8 kN (2000 lbf).   
37\. Freestanding access floors are limited to finished heights of ≤305 mm (12 in). 

 

 

 

Optical Fiber Specifications and Testing   
38\. Optical fiber cabling for PONs should be tested for bi-directional loss at 1310 nm and 1550 nm wavelengths.   
39\. APC connectors are used to mitigate reflected light in PON systems.   
40\. For optical fiber slack in the TR, store 3 m (10 ft) inside fiber hardware. 

 

Additional Design Considerations   
41\. Horizontal cabling pathways should first focus on the pathway system design.   
42\. BAS pathways should anticipate one cable per system or coverage area.   
43\. Cable trays and wireways are typically supported at 1.5 m (5 ft) centers unless designed for longer spans.   
44\. Conduit runs for horizontal pathways should follow direct routes parallel to building lines.   
45\. A third conduit bend may be acceptable without derating capacity if the conduit size is increased.   
46\. Underfloor conduit systems have limited flexibility for cable changes.

Study Notes on Chapter 8 \- Fire Protection and Firestopping   
Firestop Standards and Ratings   
1\. In the U.S., ASTM E814 or UL 1479 standards evaluate through-penetration firestop systems.   
2\. ASTM E1966 or UL 2079 standards evaluate expansion and control joints.   
3\. ASTM E119 or UL 263 standards evaluate floors, walls, beams, and structural elements.   
4\. The "F" rating measures the time a firestop resists flame passage in the U.S. and Canada.   
5\. The "T" rating measures firestop resistance to temperature rise, expressed in minutes or hours.   
6\. The "W" rating evaluates water leakage over 72 hours (U.S.).   
7\. The "L" rating measures air leakage to predict smoke movement (U.S.).   
8\. Europe uses "E" and "I" ratings equivalent to "F" and "T" ratings in the U.S.   
9\. Canadian standards require a hose stream test for FH or FTH ratings.   
10\. International "E" ratings are similar to U.S./Canada "F" ratings. 

 

Firestop Materials and Applications   
11\. Non-mechanical firestop materials include caulk, putty, and foam backer rods.   
12\. Mechanical firestop systems include cable transit systems, fire-rated pathway devices, and factory-fabricated   
sleeve systems.   
13\. Intumescent wrap strips seal materials that may burn away and leave voids.   
14\. Cementitious firestop materials are ideal for large openings.   
15\. Silicone foam firestop systems may shrink over time, causing potential hazards.   
16\. Foam backer rods must be compressed 25% thickness for firestopping.   
17\. Firestop caulk or sealant is suitable for permanent installations in small-to-medium openings.   
18\. Putty is best for small-to-medium openings with future entry requirements.   
19\. Pillows are ideal for areas anticipating frequent cable changes. 

 

 

 

Firestop Installation Guidelines   
20\. Firestop systems must consist of floor/wall assemblies, penetrating items, and firestop materials.   
21\. Substituting components in a firestop system may invalidate its rating.   
22\. Seal firestop materials symmetrically on both sides of floor/ceiling assemblies.   
23\. Electrical boxes or single cable drops penetrating one side of a barrier are membrane penetrations.   
24\. Firestop systems should balance durability, ease of installation, and maintenance.   
25\. Bags for firestopping must be installed with heavy-gauge wire mesh. 

 

Testing and Qualification   
26\. Positive pressure tests evaluate firestop resistance.   
27\. Hose stream tests in the U.S. must be conducted within 10 minutes of furnace exposure.   
28\. PASS/FAIL criteria determine flaming on the unexposed side of the assembly.   
29\. Qualification testing ensures system performance under expected conditions.   
30\. The "FT" rating in Canada is equivalent to the "T" rating in the U.S. 

 

Fire Prevention Strategies   
31\. Suppression systems, such as sprinklers, are active fire protection.   
32\. Detection systems are also considered active fire protection.   
33\. Compartmentation through fire-rated barriers is passive fire protection. 

 

Additional Firestopping Notes   
34\. Non-conductive plastic conduits easily achieve higher "T" ratings.   
35\. Factory-fabricated sleeve systems come with escutcheon plates for attachment.   
36\. Intumescent materials expand to fill gaps during a fire.   
37\. Fire-rated cables and junction boxes often require no additional firestopping.   
38\. Air leakage tests for firestop systems are optional in the U.S. 

 

Common Issues and Recommendations   
39\. Electrical apparatus penetrations in framed walls should not exceed 10,000 mm² (16 ft²) per face.   
40\. Vertical shafts with cable trays do not require firestopping within the shaft at floor assemblies.   
41\. Heavy-duty firestop systems are needed for high-risk areas like cable tray penetrations. 

 

 

 

 

 

Study Notes on Chapter 8 \- Telecommunications Grounding and Bonding   
Grounding (Earthing) Systems   
1\. Grounding provides a low-impedance path for lightning dissipation, electrostatic discharge, and high-voltage   
contact.   
2\. The ac grounding (earthing) electrode system includes:   
o Grounding electrode: Direct contact with the earth.   
o Grounding electrode conductor: Connects the system to the earth.   
3\. The ac grounding (earthing) electrode system establishes a 0 V reference for electrical systems.   
4\. The equipment grounding (earthing) system maintains 0 V on enclosures during normal operations.   
5\. The recommended maximum impedance for telecommunications equipment grounding is:   
o 0.8 ohms for 277 V ac circuits (IEEE 1100).   
o As low as possible for all other systems.   
6\. The grounding impedance is accurately measured using a ground impedance tester. 

 

Telecommunications Grounding and Bonding Components   
7\. Primary Bonding Busbar (PBB):   
o Central attachment point for grounding in the EF or ER.   
o Referenced to an approved grounding electrode.   
o   
8\. Secondary Bonding Busbar (SBB):   
o Grounding point for telecommunications systems in ERs or TRs.   
9\. Telecommunications Bonding Conductor (TBC):   
o Connects the PBB to the ac grounding electrode system.   
o Must not exceed 9 m (30 ft) in length and should measure 0 A.   
10\. Telecommunications Bonding Backbone (TBB):   
o Equalizes potentials between TRs on multiple floors.   
o Connects to the PBB at the EF.   
11\. Bonding Backbone Conductor (BBC): Equalizes potentials between TRs on the same floor. 

 

Bonding Guidelines and Standards   
12\. Telecommunications busbars should be installed near structural steel if used as a grounding electrode.   
13\. The recommended bonding resistance between a grounding electrode (e.g., steel column) and a busbar is ≤0.1   
ohms.   
14\. Bonding conductors ≤30.5 m (100 ft) should use a minimum 6 AWG conductor.   
15\. ANSI/TIA-607 and ISO/IEC 30129 specify the sizing of TBCs and BBCs to match the largest TBB. 

 

Supplementary Bonding Grid (SBG)   
16\. The SBG provides a low-impedance path between racks and cabinets in telecommunications spaces. 

 

 

17\. In raised floors, a TBC must connect the SBB to at least two points in the SBG. 

 

Isolated Ground (IG) Systems   
18\. IG systems aim to reduce EMI and RFI but may expose equipment to additional issues.   
19\. IG receptacles are often orange and not recommended for all voice and data equipment.   
20\. Field trials suggest properly installed standard three-prong grounded receptacles are more reliable than IG   
systems. 

 

Lightning Protection and Foreign Voltages   
21\. Interbuilding circuits are exposed to lightning unless:   
o Cable runs ≤42.7 m (140 ft).   
o Cables are directly buried or in underground conduits.   
22\. The lightning-protected area is within the cone of protection provided by a mast or building.   
23\. Foreign voltages are electrical currents not normally carried by telecommunications systems. 

 

Telecommunications Grounding Design Responsibilities   
24\. ICT designers:   
o Are responsible for telecommunications bonding and grounding.   
o Must identify the building's grounding electrode type.   
o Are not responsible for air terminal lightning protection (e.g., lightning rods).   
o Do not engineer surge protection systems. 

 

Safety and Performance   
25\. Grounding reduces fire hazards and enhances personnel safety.   
26\. Impedance exceeding recommended values may cause:   
o Prolonged electrical shock risks.   
o Repeated malfunctions of telecommunications equipment.   
27\. The maximum ac current on any bonding conductor should be \<1 A. 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Study Notes on Chapter 10 \- Power Distribution 

Power System Basics   
1\. Standby UPS does not filter or regulate utility power.   
2\. Real power in ac circuits is measured on the load side of the system.   
3\. Ohm’s Law applies universally in dc circuits but only to purely resistive ac circuits.   
4\. Ohm’s Law formula: P=V×I (Power \= Voltage × Current).   
5\. Voltage drop calculations use K=11K \= 11K=11 for copper circuits loaded ≤50%.   
6\. Conductor ampacity depends on size, temperature, and insulation type. 

 

Power System Redundancy and Reliability   
7\. Redundancy is expressed as N+x :   
o N+1: One backup unit.   
o N+2: Backup for two units.   
8\. Class 3 systems are concurrently maintainable.   
9\. Class 4 systems have multiple active electrical paths.   
10\. Multiple generator system topologies are used in Class 2, 3, and 4 systems.   
11\. Paralleled generator systems synchronize outputs from multiple generators. 

 

UPS Systems   
12\. Line-interactive UPS units lack frequency regulation.   
13\. Distributed redundant UPS topology consists of multiple single-module systems connected to redundant paths.   
14\. Modular UPS systems are more efficient and have a higher input power factor than static units.   
15\. Maintenance bypass completely bypasses UPS modules.   
16\. UPS sizing should include 10–25% spare capacity for future growth. 

 

 

Transformers and Noise   
18\. Transformer K-ratings:   
o K-4: Handles 35% non-linear load.   
o K-7: Handles 50% non-linear load.   
o K-13: Handles 75% non-linear load.   
19\. Power line noise categories:   
o Common-mode: Phase to ground.   
o Differential-mode: Phase to neutral.   
20\. Harmonic filters reduce voltage waveform distortion. 

 

 

 

Battery Systems   
21\. Battery classifications:   
o VRLA: Valve-regulated lead-acid, up to 3000 AH capacity.   
o Wet cell: Longer lifespan than VRLA.   
o Lithium-ion: Longest lifespan.   
22\. Battery life and capacity are influenced by temperature:   
o Low temperatures decrease capacity but increase lifespan.   
23\. Battery protection devices interrupt short circuit currents (≈10× AH rating).   
24\. Battery racks and cabinets require 1 m (3.3 ft) clearance per AHJ standards. 

 

Alarms and Faults   
25\. Power system alarms:   
o Major alarms: Low voltage, undervoltage.   
o Minor alarms: Overcurrent.   
26\. Arc flashes generate temperatures 4× that of the sun’s surface. 

 

 

 

Power Waveforms and Reactance   
27\. Continuous dc, pulsed dc, sinusoidal ac, and offset ac waveforms are used in Class 4 circuits.   
28\. In ac circuits with capacitive reactance, current leads to voltage.   
29\. Phase difference is measured by dividing the time for one cycle into 360°. 

 

Surge Protection and Static Transfer Switches   
30\. Surge arresters limit surge voltages by bypassing or discharging surge current.   
31\. Static Transfer Switches (STS):   
o Transfer load between two synchronized power sources.   
o Typical transfer time: ¼ cycle. 

 

Cooling and Ventilation   
32\. Cooling requirements:   
o 3517 watts of power requires 1 ton of air conditioning.   
33\. Battery venting requires 4 air volume changes per hour. 

 

 

 

Electrical Distribution   
34\. Neutral conductors carry current and may handle up to 200% rated load in non-linear devices.   
35\. Sizing of electrical equipment like transformers and panelboards is based on apparent power.   
36\. For three-phase panels, balanced loads cancel currents on the neutral conductor. 

 

Faults and Power Issues   
37\. Definitions:   
o Sag: Decrease in voltage for 0.5 cycle to 1 minute.   
o Undervoltage: Decrease \>1 minute.   
o Overvoltage: Increase \>1 minute.   
o Interruption: Total absence of voltage on one or more phase conductors. 

 

Miscellaneous   
38\. PoE cables deliver Class 2 power loads.   
39\. Remote alarms for power systems include cabled and dial-up options.   
40\. NFPA 70E provides arc flash hazard safety standards.

Study Notes on Chapter 11 \- Field Testing of Structured Cabling   
Balanced Twisted-Pair Cabling Testing   
1\. Insertion Loss: Measures signal loss between a transmitter and receiver due to the insertion of a component,   
link, or channel.   
2\. Return Loss: Indicates impedance uniformity and mismatch; smaller decibel loss means better performance.   
3\. NEXT Loss: Measures signal coupling between two pairs; higher values indicate better performance.   
4\. ACR-F: Crosstalk measured at the opposite end from the disturbing signal, normalized by attenuation loss;   
larger values are better.   
5\. Propagation Delay: Delays vary for each pair.   
6\. Wire Map Test: Verifies correct conductor connections, checks for grounded conductors, shield continuity,   
and identifies transposed, reversed, or split pairs.   
7\. DC Loop Resistance: Measures conductor resistance with the far end shorted.   
8\. Power Sum Crosstalk: Required for applications communicating over multiple pairs simultaneously.   
9\. Alien Crosstalk: Noise caused by adjacent data communication cables.   
10\. Testing Equipment:   
o Level III field test instruments required for category 6A and class EA cabling.   
o TDR used for length calculation and fault identification.   
11\. Standards Compliance: Use shortest electrical length for pass/fail decisions. 

 

Optical Fiber Cabling Testing   
1\. Insertion Loss: Tested with a light source and power meter or OLTS.   
2\. Attenuation: Expressed in dB; system loss must be less than the link loss budget.   
3\. OTDR Testing:   
o Used to measure length, attenuation, and characterize quality.   
o Requires a launch cord and a receive cord, each longer than the OTDR's dead zone.   
o Trace forms a slope from left to right, steeper at shorter wavelengths.   
4\. Attenuation Coefficients:   
o Worst-case for 50/125 µm laser-optimized OM3 at 850 nm: 3.0 dB/km.   
o Worst-case for singlemode at 1550 nm: 1.0 dB/km.   
5\. Polarity Testing: Verified with OLTS or a visible light source.   
6\. Splice Attenuation: Total loss calculated as the number of splices multiplied by individual splice loss   
(typically 0.3 dB).   
7\. Multimode Testing: OTDR complements OLTS measurements; operates at 1300 nm ± 20 nm.   
8\. Singlemode Testing: Operates at 1310 nm ± 10 nm or 1550 nm ± 20 nm. 

 

 

 

Coaxial Cabling Testing   
1\. Key Parameters:   
o Voltage Standing Wave Ratio (VSWR).   
o TDR impedance.   
o Signal strength (spectrum analyzer).   
o Output level vs. spurious signals.   
2\. Required Devices:   
o 50-ohm cable tester.   
o Spectrum analyzer. 

 

Additional Notes   
• Test Preparation: Post-installation testing must follow cable reel testing within five days of delivery.   
• Insertion Loss Deviation: Not required as an acceptance test.   
• Optical Length: Can exceed physical length in cables like stranded loose tube due to fiber lay.   
• Power Sum AXT: Captured via PSANEXT and PSAACR-F. 

 

 

 

Study Notes on Chapter 12 \- Outside Plant (OSP) 

General Guidelines   
1\. Intrabuilding Conduit Penetration: Specified range is 25 mm (1 in) to 77 mm (3 in).   
2\. Underground Building Entry: When the entrance isn’t on the property line, provide two conduits from inside   
the building to the property line or easement.   
3\. Direct-Buried Cables:   
o Easily bypass obstructions.   
o Must be placed below the 50-year frost line in cold regions.   
o Inflexible for future service reinforcement or changes.   
4\. Aerial Plant:   
o Designed for placement on the roadside of pole lines.   
o Building entrances limited to small buildings needing ≤72-strand optical fiber cables.   
o Span from the last pole to the building must not exceed 30.5 m (100 ft).   
5\. Conduit Specifications:   
o Must extend to undisturbed earth to prevent shearing.   
o Top of the conduit must be at least 610 mm (24 in) below ground surface.   
o Entrance sleeves must slope downward toward the building exterior.

1\. EF Terminations:   
o Require void-free plywood backboards, 2.4 m (8 ft) high, 19 mm (0.75 in) thick.   
o Conduits terminate 101.6 mm (4 in) from finished walls inside EF.   
2\. Conduit Pathway Design:   
o Include a minimum of four conduits, plus one spare, with a trade size of 4\.   
o Total bends in the pathway must not exceed 189 degrees between pulling points.   
o Pull cords must be installed in all conduits and innerducts.   
3\. Maintenance Holes (MHs):   
o Equipped with sumps at least 330 mm (13 in) in diameter.   
o Main conduit entry points should be at opposite ends.   
o Configurations vary, and each is identified with a one-letter designation. 

 

Loading and Protection   
1\. Pole Loading:   
o Communications poles must withstand the same horizontal load applied 610 mm (24 in) from the top.   
o Vertical conduit masts cannot extend more than 101.6 mm (4 in) above the roofline.   
o Weakest-rated pole for aerial plant is Class 10\.   
2\. Barriers:   
o Posts or bollards for protection must extend 1067 mm (42 in) above finished grade. 

 

Installation Methods   
1\. Buried Installations:   
o Use compact trenchers or vibratory plows for minimal surface disruption.   
o Stepped trenches deeper than 1.52 m (5 ft) require multiple steps.   
2\. Joint Trench Method:   
o Separation requirements:   
▪ 305 mm (12 in) between telecommunications and water pipes (parallel).   
▪ 77 mm (3 in) of concrete between telecommunications and power conduits.   
▪ 152 mm (6 in) between telecommunications and gas pipes (crossing).   
3\. Directional Drilling:   
o Valuable for areas with difficult excavation, such as under buildings or rivers. 

 

Diversity and Service   
1\. Service Diversity:   
o Cable diversity assigns circuits among different sheaths/cables.   
o Route diversity assigns circuits along entirely different paths to a building.   
2\. Network Interface (NI):   
o Point of connection between service provider and client networks.   
o Interior applications may have separate hardware for NI and active equipment. 

 

 

 

Additional Recommendations   
1\. Color Coding:   
o Orange warning tape should be buried above telecommunications and CATV cables.   
2\. Aerial Duct:   
o "Figure eight" ducts include built-in support strands.   
3\. Maintenance Space:   
o Terminals require 1 m (3.28 ft) of clearance on all sides for access.   
4\. Surface-Mounted Cabinets:

AV Design and Layout

Study Notes on Chapter 14 \- Intelligent Building systems 

General Overview   
1\. System Hierarchy:   
o Management-Level Processor: Collects, stores, and processes historical data (e.g., energy usage,   
operating costs, alarm reports).   
o System-Level Controllers: Interface directly with controlled equipment through cabled sensors or   
indirectly via communication links.   
o Field-Level Controllers: Designed for Direct Digital Control (DDC) of unitary equipment and   
HVAC, EMS, and access control applications.   
2\. Control and Communication:   
o BAS devices are power-limited and communicate using low-speed protocols.   
o BAS communication links typically use poll-response or peer protocol.   
o Modern BAS systems utilize distributed intelligence for efficiency. 

 

Cabling and Connections   
1\. Cabling Standards:   
o Balanced twisted-pair conductors (16 AWG to 22 AWG) are commonly used.   
o Horizontal connection points (HCP) should be at least 15.2 m (50 ft) from the telecommunications   
room (TR).   
o BAS signals and telecommunications signals should not share the same binder group in a multi-pair   
backbone cable.   
o Cross-connections for BAS services (especially FA) should use red insulator clips for identification.   
o BAS communication bus lengths can extend up to 1219.2 m (4000 ft) without extenders or repeaters.   
2\. Coverage Area:   
o One BAS device per 23 m² (248 ft²) for commercial office buildings or hotel rooms.   
o Coverage area connections are reconfigured at the HCP, with 12 links max per HCP.   
o No more than one HCP should be placed in a single horizontal cabling link. 

 

HVAC and EMS Integration   
1\. Control Types:   
o Automatic Controls: For variables like temperature and humidity.   
o Limit Controls: Prevent freezing of water coils or heat exchangers.   
o Pneumatic Controls: Use air pressure from sensors for input.   
o Daylight Harvesting: Saves \~4% energy in average buildings.   
o Lighting controls account for up to 40% of electrical operational costs in commercial buildings.   
2\. EMS Programs:   
o Optimum Start: Minimizes HVAC runtime by calculating occupancy temperature needs.   
o Load Reset: Ensures minimum energy usage for cooling or heating.   
o Night Cycle: Maintains temperature limits during unoccupied periods. 

 

 

 

Design and Installation Guidelines   
1\. Equipment Placement:   
o System-level controllers and cabling can be installed in mechanical/electrical areas.   
o BAS outlets are generally not required when using HCPs.   
2\. Labeling and Identification:   
o Balanced twisted-pair connections for FA systems should avoid RJ-series patch cords and use distinct   
red insulator clips.   
o Each horizontal and backbone cabling link should be properly labeled and routed.   
3\. Upgrades:   
o BAS products and technologies are typically upgraded every 5 to 7 years. 

 

Specialized Functions   
1\. Fire Alarm System Integration:   
o BAS sequences include elevator recall and shutdown.   
2\. Energy Management:   
o Integral to mid-sized to large buildings' HVAC systems.   
o Management processors handle energy usage analysis and cost reporting.   
3\. Additional Functions:   
o BAS can include controls for window shades, digital signage, and more. 

 

Efficiency and Future-Proofing   
• BAS integration with telecommunications cabling centralizes controllers while supporting distributed   
processing.   
• Horizontal and backbone cabling designs ensure scalability and flexibility for future system enhancements. 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Study Notes on Chapter 16 \- Wireless Networks 

General Wireless Concepts   
1\. IEEE Standards:   
o Wi-Fi Standards: Defined under IEEE 802.11, covering Wi-Fi 1 through Wi-Fi 6\.   
o Wi-Fi 6: IEEE 802.11ax offers a 400% improvement in throughput compared to Wi-Fi 5\.   
o Personal Area Networks (PANs): Defined by IEEE 802.15, typically within a 10 m (33 ft) radius.   
o Power over Ethernet (PoE):   
▪ Mode A: Delivers power over multiple pairs in a 4-pair cable.   
▪ Mode B: Delivers power over unused pairs in a 4-pair cable.   
2\. Wireless Networks Types:   
o Personal Area Network (PAN): Interconnects devices in a personal space.   
o Wireless Local Area Network (WLAN):   
▪ Basic Service Set (BSS): Single AP manages communication.   
▪ Independent BSS (IBSS): Stations communicate directly without an AP.   
▪ Extended Service Set (ESS): Multiple APs linked to expand coverage.   
o Distributed Antenna System (DAS): Extends RF coverage using distributed antennas.   
3\. Security:   
o Encryption Standards:   
▪ AES for WPANs.   
▪ WPA/WPA2 for WLANs (WEP is not recommended).   
o Advanced signal processing with MIMO enables data to multiple devices simultaneously. 

 

Distributed Antenna Systems (DAS)   
1\. Cabling Types:   
o Five cabling types are recognized, including:   
▪ Category 6A/Class EA for balanced twisted-pair systems.   
▪ Radiating Coax (Leaky Coax): Operates from 70 MHz to 2.4 GHz.   
2\. System Design:   
o Point-to-Point: For long-range transmission.   
o Point-to-Multipoint: For in-building applications.   
o Hybrid Couplers (Crossband Couplers): Facilitate RF signal management.   
3\. Link Budget:   
o Refers to the sum of gains and losses in the system, including transmission line loss and antenna gains.   
4\. Components:   
o Backend equipment located in telecommunications rooms.   
o Non-radiating coax and splitters/couplers used in traditional DAS setups. 

 

Wireless Transmission Principles   
1\. Beamforming:   
o Enhances RF signal strength using three methods:   
▪ Precoding: Same signal through multiple antennas.   
▪ Diversity Coding: Orthogonal signals through multiple antennas.   
▪ Spatial Multiplexing: Splits high-bandwidth RF into low-bandwidth signals.   
2\. Duplex Transceivers: 

 

 

o Time Division Duplex (TDD): Transmits/receives alternately in time slots.   
o Frequency Division Duplex (FDD): Transmits/receives simultaneously on separate frequencies.   
3\. Path Loss:   
o The reduction in RF signal strength as it travels from the origin to the endpoint. 

 

Wireless Frequency Bands and Channels   
1\. Unlicensed Spectrum:   
o Frequencies: 2.4 GHz, 5 GHz, and 6 GHz.   
o 2.4 GHz traditionally used for indoor multi-point networks.   
o 5 GHz range divided into 23 channels, each 20 MHz wide.   
2\. Regulations:   
o Channels 5.150-5.250 GHz and 5.735-5.815 GHz are always permitted.   
o Channels 5.170-5.740 GHz restricted when in use by government devices.   
3\. Climate Effects:   
o High frequencies (e.g., above 6 GHz) are affected by rain fade and heavy moisture. 

 

WLAN Infrastructure and Equipment   
1\. Access Points (APs):   
o Centralized communication and management for WLANs.   
o Beamforming and MIMO enhance coverage and performance.   
2\. WLAN Controllers:   
o Centrally manage connected APs in enterprise environments.   
3\. WLAN Gateways:   
o Facilitate communication between different networks. 

 

Outdoor Wireless Systems   
1\. Antenna Support Structures:   
o Additional considerations for structures exceeding 60 m (200 ft) in height.   
o Radome covers for parabolic antennas may cause 0.5 dB path loss.   
2\. RF Media:   
o Flexible/rigid waveguides provide low-loss transmission for frequencies above 1 GHz.   
o RF media on rooftops should be supported 38 mm (1.5 in) above the surface.   
3\. Design Challenges:   
o Environmental factors like rain fade and signal interference. 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Wireless Innovations and Advancements   
1\. Wi-Fi 6 Features:   
o Enhanced multi-user support with multi-user MIMO.   
o Higher throughput and improved performance in dense environments.   
2\. Digital and Analog Signal Handling:   
o Digital signals use time division duplex transceivers.   
o Analog signals use automatic gain control (AGC) in active DAS.   
3\. Infrared Communication (IR):   
o Line-of-sight transmission with a maximum allowable illumination of 10 lux. 

 

Study Notes on Chapter 17 \- Electronic Access Control (EAC) 

Access Control Systems   
1\. Access Devices and Credentials:   
o Examples of credentials include:   
▪ Access cards, magnetic stripe cards, fingerprints, facial recognition, and passwords.   
▪ Physical attributes (biometric) or objects (e.g., key fobs).   
o EAC Levels:   
▪ Level 3: Includes field devices such as credential readers and locking mechanisms.   
2\. Lock Types and Functions:   
o Lock Types:   
▪ Cylindrical Locks: Mounted through the door.   
▪ Mortise Locks: Embedded into the door and latch to a strike in the doorframe.   
▪ Electric Bolts: Offer high security.   
▪ Magnetic Door Locks: Typically fail-safe.   
o Lock Functions:   
▪ Entrance Lock: Inside lever always allows egress, outside lever can be locked/unlocked via   
key.   
▪ Vestibule Lock: Inside lever always allows egress, outside lever's access is key-controlled.   
3\. Door Hardware:   
o Electric Strikes: Fail-secure; often include monitoring switches like latch bolt monitors.   
o Electrified Exit Hardware (Panic Hardware): Commonly used in double-door applications. 

 

Fire Alarm Control Panels (FACP)   
1\. Pathway Designations:   
o Class A: Redundant signal pathway; continues to function past a single open circuit.   
o Class B: No redundancy; stops at a single open circuit.   
o Class N: Ethernet or network protocol-based pathways.   
o Class C: Used with addressable devices and appliances.   
o Class E: Not monitored for integrity, used for supplemental equipment.   
2\. Shared Pathways:   
o Level 1: Life safety data prioritized over non-life safety data.   
o Level 0: No segregation or prioritization of life safety data. 

 

 

 

3\. Alarm Systems:   
o Digital Alarm Communicator Transmitter (DACT):   
▪ Must connect to two separate transmission channels.   
▪ Calls forwarded lines must verify integrity every 4 hours.   
o Digital Alarm Radio Transmitter (DART):   
▪ Provides backup if DACT fails.   
▪ Must complete test signals at least once every 24 hours.   
4\. NFPA 72 Standards:   
o Public fire alarm signaling must achieve 15 dB above ambient sound levels. 

 

Video Surveillance Systems (VSS)   
1\. Analog Systems:   
o Traditional analog systems use a cross-point matrix for control and display.   
o Video is transmitted to the matrix at all times, viewed or not.   
2\. Digital Systems:   
o Frame Rate: Minimum acceptable rate is 3.75 fps.   
o IR Cameras: Also called starlight cameras; used for low-light conditions.   
3\. Lenses:   
o Common lens types exclude focal lenses.   
4\. Video Compression:   
o MPEG-2 is commonly used for digital TV signal transmission. 

 

Event Monitoring and Mitigation Techniques   
1\. Event-Based Monitoring:   
o High integration between systems for operational response.   
o Example: Fire alarms triggering elevator shutdown.   
2\. Mitigation Techniques:   
o Electronic: Credential readers, cameras, electrified locks.   
o Architectural: Crime prevention through design.   
o Operational: Guard posts, patrols, and emergency preparedness. 

 

Security and System Design   
1\. Threat Analysis:   
o Identifies sources of risk to mitigate threats to data, property, and well-being.   
o Divides potential damage into seven categories.   
2\. Network Design:   
o Exclusive Communication Networks: EAC data on dedicated physical networks.   
o Common topologies include loop and star.   
3\. Lock Hardware Grades:   
o Grade 1: Most durable, used in high-security applications. 

 

 

 

Electronic Security Systems (ESS)   
1\. Integration:   
o ESS integrates video surveillance, access control, and alarm systems.   
2\. Credentials:   
o ESS uses credentials for access, including physical objects and biometric data.   
3\. Design:   
o Includes programmed access level changes and shared pathways for alarms and monitoring. 

 

Study Notes on Chapter 18 \- Data Centers    
Data Center Infrastructure and Standards   
1\. Facility Availability Classes:   
o Class F0: Targeted availability \< 90%; \> 400 hours annual maintenance window.   
o Class F1: 99% availability; 100–400 hours annual maintenance window; critical power uses power   
conditioning.   
o Class F2: 99.9% availability; up to 99 hours annual maintenance; redundancy for critical components   
only.   
o Class F3: 99.99% availability; 49 hours annual maintenance; redundancy may exist without   
component redundancy.   
o Class F4: 99.999% availability; systems are automated to reduce human error.   
2\. Cabling Standards:   
o ANSI/TIA-568: Category 6 minimum for horizontal cabling.   
o CENELEC and ISO/IEC: Class EA for zone, intermediate, and main distribution cabling; OM3   
minimum for multimode fiber.   
o Cable Management: Planned capacity should be 40% by cross-sectional area; max pathway fill is   
50%.   
o Backbone Cabling: Equivalent to "network access cabling" in CENELEC/ISO standards.   
3\. Redundancy:   
o N+1: Failure or maintenance of one unit does not disrupt operations.   
o N+2: Failure or maintenance of two units does not disrupt operations.   
o 2N: Two complete systems for every one required. 

 

Data Center Security   
1\. Four Concentric Layers of Security:   
o 1\. Site Security: Protects the data center perimeter.   
o 2\. Building and Access Control Security: Controls access to facilities.   
o 3\. Computer Room Security: Ensures only authorized personnel access sensitive areas.   
o 4\. Information Security: Protects stored and processed data.   
2\. Authentication Factors:   
o Primary: Something you know (password), have (access card), or are (biometrics).   
o Optional: Behavioral authentication (e.g., typing patterns). 

 

 

 

Design Considerations   
1\. Room Design:   
o Lighting located above aisles, adequate illumination for maintenance.   
o Overhead cable trays must be at least 2.75 m (9 ft) above the finished floor.   
2\. Equipment Cabinets:   
o Cabinets without fan-assisted cooling should have at least 63% open door space.   
o Blade servers and high-density systems often use top-of-rack switches.   
3\. Telecommunications Cabling:   
o OM3 fiber supports:   
▪ 400G Ethernet: 8 or 32 fibers.   
▪ 200G Ethernet: 8 fibers.   
▪ 50G Ethernet: 2 fibers.   
o Category 6/Class E or higher is recommended. 

 

Energy Efficiency and Maintenance   
1\. Energy Metrics:   
o HVAC Efficiency: Greatest opportunity for energy savings.   
o Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE): Common benchmark for energy efficiency.   
2\. Maintenance:   
o Annual maintenance windows vary by class:   
▪ F0: \>400 hours   
▪ F1: 100–400 hours   
▪ F2: Up to 99 hours   
▪ F3: 49 hours   
o Maintenance during unscheduled hours in F2 and below. 

 

Structured Cabling Hierarchies   
1\. Distributors:   
o Main Distributor (MD): Connects to external networks.   
o Intermediate Distributor (ID): Links zones within the facility.   
o Zone Distributor (ZD): Distributes to specific areas.   
2\. Subsystems:   
o Network Access Cabling Subsystem: Connects entrance rooms to distributors.   
o Zone Distribution Cabling Subsystem: Links horizontal and equipment distribution areas. 

 

Threats and Risk Mitigation   
1\. Threats to Sensitive Data:   
o Physical threats, including human intervention, pose the greatest risks.   
2\. Site Selection: 

 

 

o Considerations: Crime, natural disasters, utilities, emergency services.   
3\. Survivability:   
o Protects against external events to minimize downtime. 

 

Study Notes on Chapter 19 \- Health Care   
Key Concepts in Health Care Communications   
1\. Technology Equipment Centers (TEC):   
o Exclusively for local data storage, processing, and networking.   
o Located below the top floor and at least 3.7 m (12 ft) from X-ray or EMI sources.   
o Must maintain temperature (18–24°C / 64–75°F) and relative humidity (30–55%).   
o Houses main networking equipment, application servers, and data storage devices.   
2\. Technology Distribution Rooms (TDR):   
o At least one TDR is required per floor.   
o Maximum horizontal cable distance to the work area: 90 m (295 ft).   
o Connected to the TEC for building-wide communication.   
o Maintains similar environmental controls as TECs. 

 

Nurse Call Systems   
1\. Components:   
o Initiation Devices: Staff emergency stations, bedside stations, bathroom stations, pillow speakers.   
o Notification Devices: Corridor lights, annunciator panels, zone lights, master stations, paging   
systems.   
o Headend Equipment: Processes and routes alarms/signals.   
2\. Standards and Wiring:   
o Governed by manufacturers and must comply with UL 1069 standards.   
o Electrically isolated from other systems.   
3\. Design Considerations:   
o Corridor Lights: Installed at intersections of corridors in nursing units.   
o Audiovisual Systems: Used where supervision and emergency responses are critical.   
o Tamper-Resistance Hall Stations: Allowed only in audiovisual systems. 

 

Radio Frequency Identification (RFID)   
1\. Active vs. Passive Systems:   
o Active RFID: Typical range of 1–2 m (3.28–6.6 ft); operates at 2.4–5.8 GHz.   
o Passive RFID: Operates at 902–928 MHz (UHF); trackable in real-time.   
2\. Uses:   
o Tracking patients, staff, and assets.   
o Newer ultra-wideband systems operate in the 3.1–10 GHz range. 

 

 

 

Wireless Medical Telemetry Services (WMTS)   
• Frequency: Dedicated range of 608–614 MHz for transmitting patient vitals.   
• Licensing: No individual license required in the U.S. for operation. 

 

Infrastructure Requirements   
1\. Entrance Facility (EF):   
o Houses data/voice circuit entry points.   
o Can be combined with the TEC in smaller facilities.   
o Connected to emergency power systems.   
2\. HVAC:   
o Must be connected to emergency power.   
o Supports critical spaces like the EF and TEC.   
3\. Operating Rooms (ORs):   
o Among the most technology-dependent areas.   
o Requires careful design for speaker systems and volume control. 

 

Real-Time Locating Systems (RTLS)   
• Types: Active or passive tracking systems.   
• Usage: Real-time tracking of patients, staff, and equipment. 

 

Communication Applications in Health Care   
1\. Picture Archiving and Communication Systems (PACS):   
o Used for storing and viewing digital radiology images.   
2\. In-Patient Television Systems (IPTS):   
o Can utilize new or existing TV distribution systems.   
o Monitor placement depends on the headwall or footwall design. 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

 

Study Notes on Chapter 20 \- Residential Cabling Systems   
Key Concepts in Residential Cabling 

 

Residential Cabling Grades   
1\. Grade 1:   
o Minimum system for basic telecommunications services.   
o Cabling Includes:   
▪ One Category 6 balanced twisted-pair cable.   
▪ One 75-ohm Series-6 coaxial cable.   
2\. Grade 2:   
o Supports current and advanced multimedia telecommunications services.   
o Cabling Includes:   
▪ Two Category 6 balanced twisted-pair cables.   
▪ Two 75-ohm Series-6 coaxial cables.   
▪ May include a two-strand multimode optical fiber cable.   
3\. Grade 3:   
o Exceeds requirements for current and developing advanced telecommunications services.   
o Cabling Includes:   
▪ Balanced twisted-pair cables.   
▪ Coaxial cables.   
▪ Multimode optical fiber cables (minimum of two strands).   
o Optional Components:   
▪ Speaker cabling.   
▪ Security cabling. 

 

Topologies   
• Twisted-Pair and Optical Fiber Cabling:   
o Implemented in a star topology.   
• Coaxial Cabling:   
o May be implemented in bus, star, or both. 

 

Distribution Device (DD)   
1\. Placement:   
o Should not be installed on exterior walls.   
o Requires a dedicated 15-ampere, 120-volt AC outlet within 1.52 m (5 ft).   
2\. Height Requirements:   
o Depends on the number of outlets/connectors:   
▪ Grade 1 with up to 8 outlets: 254–457 mm (10–18 in).   
▪ Grade 2 with up to 8 outlets: 457–914 mm (18–36 in).   
▪ Grade 2 with 17–24 outlets: 711–1067 mm (28–42 in).   
▪ Installations with more than 24 outlets: Multiple interconnected DDs. 

 

 

3\. Future Proofing:   
o Allow for 20–50% additional cable runs for future growth. 

 

Auxiliary Disconnect Outlet (ADO)   
• Provides disconnection between the living unit and the access provider.   
• Minimum Requirements:   
o Coaxial: One coaxial cable per living unit.   
o Twisted-Pair: Two 4-pair cables per living unit.   
o Optical Fiber: Two strands per living unit.   
• Co-located with the DD. 

 

Residential Design for Multi-Dwelling Units (MDUs)   
1\. Common Telecommunications Room (CTR):   
o Serves backbone and ADO cable terminations.   
o Minimum space for 5 tenant units:   
▪ Grade 2: 775 mm (30.5 in) wide and 610 mm (24 in) high.   
▪ Grade 3: 775 mm (30.5 in) wide and 610 mm (24 in) high.   
2\. Backbone Cabling:   
o Twisted-Pair: Star topology.   
o Coaxial: Bus and/or star topology.   
3\. Location Recommendations:   
o CTR should be located on each floor or every third floor.   
o Co-located with the entrance facility (EF) when possible. 

 

Testing and Verification   
1\. Verification Testing:   
o Two stages:   
▪ After cabling installation (rough-in stage, before drywall).   
▪ During the termination stage (after painting).   
2\. Qualification Testing:   
o Preferred for voice and data cabling during termination.   
3\. Certification Testing:   
o May replace verification and qualification testing during the trim-out stage. 

 

General Design Recommendations   
• Cable Length:   
o Maximum twisted-pair outlet cable length: 90 m (295 ft).   
o Includes allowance for 10 m (33 ft) of patch cords, jumpers, or equipment cords. 

 

 

• Living Units:   
o Each living area should have at least one telecommunications outlet.   
o Not all outlets need identical configurations.   
• Grounding:   
o Building telecommunications ground must be within 1.52 m (5 ft) of the DD. 

 

Special Considerations   
• Antenna Entrances:   
o May be included in the EF for MDUs.   
• Network Interface Devices (NID):   
o Should not be used as a DD.   
• Administration:   
o Should follow the same principles as commercial buildings for MDUs.

Study Notes on Chapter 22 \- Special Design Considerations 

 

Key Standards and Guidelines   
• Standards for Special Occupancy Locations:   
o TIA-1005 and ISO/IEC 11801-3 provide design guidelines for environments outside typical   
commercial premises.   
o Allow for specialized cabling such as:   
▪ Single Pair Ethernet (SPE) cabling per ISO/IEC 61156\.   
▪ 2-pair, 100-ohm balanced twisted-pair cabling.   
▪ LC connectors for optical fiber cabling (TIA-604-10A). 

 

Environmental Classifications and Ratings   
MICE Classification System   
• Describes Mechanical, Ingress, Climatic/Chemical, and Electromagnetic (MICE) conditions.   
• Classifications:   
o MICE 1: Commercial environments.   
o MICE 2: Light industrial environments.   
o MICE 3: Harsh industrial environments.   
• Industrial control system environments:   
o Work areas and machine areas are the harshest.   
Ingress Protection (IP) Code   
• IP codes denote resistance to foreign objects and water:   
o Format: IPxy.   
▪ x: Protection from solid objects.   
▪ y: Protection from water.   
o Examples:   
▪ x \= 0: No protection.   
▪ x \= 5: Partial protection from dust.   
▪ x \= 6: Dust-tight.   
▪ y \= 1: Vertically falling water.   
▪ y \= 4: Splashed water from all directions.   
▪ y \= 7: Immersion in water up to 1 meter for 30 minutes.   
NEMA Ratings   
• Indoor enclosures with specific protections:   
o NEMA 1: Falling dirt.   
o NEMA 4X: Corrosive agents.   
o NEMA 12K: Oil and coolant seepage.   
o NEMA 6P: Water and prolonged exposure. 

 

 

 

Hazardous and Climatic Conditions   
Hazardous Locations   
• Examples:   
o Battery rooms.   
o Vehicle fueling and repair facilities.   
o Areas with chemically reactive materials (e.g., food and beverage processing facilities).   
• Must comply with codes such as NEC (NFPA 70).   
Climatic Conditions   
• Areas with temperature or humidity levels outside normal human occupancy.   
• Examples:   
o Airplane hangars.   
o Toll and fare collection sites.   
o Ventilation plants. 

 

Industrial Areas   
Defined Areas in Industrial Premises   
• Three primary areas as per TIA-1005 and ISO/IEC 11801-3:   
1\. Control Rooms.   
2\. Automation Islands:   
▪ Machinery installations without personnel during operation.   
3\. Work Areas.   
Applications   
• Structured cabling supports:   
o Voice and data systems.   
o Process and production control systems.   
o Equipment for industrial environments. 

 

Special Design Requirements   
Design Considerations   
• Compliance with environmental standards and applicable codes.   
• MICE classification to guide material selection.   
• IP and NEMA ratings for enclosures.   
Pathways and Spaces 

 

 

• Subject to additional requirements for harsh environments.   
• Specific needs for systems like:   
o Process control.   
o Production systems.   
Connectivity   
• Optical fiber cabling:   
o Star, ring, or bus topology.   
o LC connectors as standard.   
• SPE cabling for unique applications. 

 

 

Key Takeaways   
• Special occupancy designs must account for unique hazards and severe environmental conditions.   
• Use MICE classifications, IP codes, and NEMA ratings to ensure compatibility with the environment.   
• Compliance with TIA-1005 and ISO/IEC 11801-3 standards ensures robust, safe designs tailored for   
industrial and harsh environments.
